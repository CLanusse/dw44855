Biography

Arvo Pärt was born on September 11, 1935 in Paide. He graduated from the composition class of Heino Eller at the Tallinn State Conservatory and worked as a sound director on Estonian Radio. Since the end of the 1960s, Pärt has been a freelance composer.

Both the avant-garde spirit of Pärt's early work and the religious nature of the works completed in the 1970s led to controversial criticism and caused a number of confrontations with the authorities at the time. In 1980, the Pärt family was forced to emigrate - first to Vienna, then to Berlin, where they stayed for almost 30 years.

In 1984, the composer's collaboration with Manfred Eicher, the founder of the renowned record company ECM, began. In the same year, under the auspices of ECM, Pärt's album "Tabula rasa" was released, the success of which brought listeners to his work all over the world. Pärt's music quickly reached the repertoire of well-known orchestras and ensembles, and from there to many records. In 2010, Arvo Pärt returned to Estonia.

As a composer, Pärt attracted attention already in the 1960s, when he became one of the leading figures of avant-garde music both in Estonia and in the entire Soviet Union. Many of the most important modernist compositional techniques of the 20th century entered the music scene here through Pärt's works, such as "Obituary", "Perpetuum mobile", "Pro et contra" and others.

The dramatic collage "Credo", created in 1968, became a turning point in his work and life, after which the composer abandoned the composition techniques used so far. The search for a new sound language led to Pärt's creative crisis, which lasted eight years. During this time he converted to Orthodoxy and also devoted himself to the study of Gregorian chant, late medieval polyphony and Renaissance music.

In 1976, Pärt reached his original musical language, which he named tintinnabulum ( tintinnabulum - "bell" in Latin). The first work written in the new sound language was a small piano piece for Aliina (1976), followed by Cantus in Memory of Benjamin Britten (1977), Fratres (1977), Tabula Rasa (1977), Mirror in the Mirror (1978) and many other works by which the composer is best known today.

Arvo Pärt's diverse work includes orchestral music, including four symphonies, works for choir and orchestra, soloists and orchestra, a cappella choral music, chamber music and organ works. Most of them are based on liturgical or other spiritual texts, such as the large forms Passio (1982), Te Deum (1985), Miserere (1989/1992), Kanon pokajanen (1997) and Adam's Lament (2010).
